Overview

This page is documentation for the interaction prototype for a new School of Design website and intranet. At the bottom of the page is a diagram of which pages are presently functional in this prototype.

The intent of this page is to highlight insights that we learned over the course of a semester researching the school and its constituencies, as well as document the various interaction and visual standards that we established and our thought processes behind them.

Key Insights

  • Importance of Information

    The website is the main source of information about the school and should be treated as a priority.

    Information should be shown in the format that it wants to be in. Information should be considered and carefully designed.

    The web is capable of good typography and hierarchy and should reflect the aesthetic values of the school.

  • Process Culture

    The importance of process and documentation should be indoctrinated in the students. We present stories, not slideshows.

    Students should be encouraged to think about how to talk about their work. Next steps could include how to enable conversation and even critique.

    The new gallery structure isn't creating more work, it's a natural step forward from physical process books. It's more environmental and accessible for everyone.

  • Involving the Community

    All parties we've spoken to agree that there is not enough transparency and overlap between different groups within the school.

    Students generating content for the Exhibition alleviates work from professors, and gives students a voice in how their work is depicted.

    Given an outlet, students will create high-quality content that drives internal, and consequently external, culture.

Brand notes

The current expression CMU Design's brand is misaligned with what we experience as members of the school. Students, faculty, and visitors know we have an incredible program that approaches its goals with sincerity and is blessed with a wealth of talent. Prospectives and outsiders see a dated institution with a shallow sense of community despite its fantastic alum. We think the website is an opportunity to foster a vibrant community.

Imagine going to the CMU Design website, and seing featured "op-ed" content from its community: an illustrated guide to design school by a worldy senior, a video made by students documenting a camping excursion, a guide to upcoming PGH concerts and events—all written by students and vetted by faculty. There's no substitute for community when building a strong brand, and the results will speak for themselves—in demand from prospectives, faculty searches, and alumni donations.

RISD already has a student run publication, and we've shown initiative too. Imagine if students knew they had a voice on the website and could get more eyeballs on their content. To capture this sense of community to strengthen our brand, all we have to do is put it on the front page of our website.

Exhibition and Intranet

The Exhibition is our implementation of a Gallery. It is based around classes and projects, with every student's work online. All students are required in the curriculum to document their work digitally on the intranet, and then faculty and staff revise submissions before any work goes live.

The public sees work from the past semester, allowing maintenance of the Exhibition to be completed anytime before the semester deadline. Internally, members of the School would be able to view the entire archive of work via the internet.

The Exhibition also provides opportunities for students to have their work highlighted. Highlighted work appears in the heroes and sidekicks of exhibition pages. The front page of the Exhibition features one or a few exemplary projects. Class-level pages house one piece of work that represents the goals of that class, and likewise for project-level pages.


The Intranet is designed to be nondisruptive with people's current habits and straightforward to implement and adopt. The Presto printing service (pit.design.cmu.edu) is folded into the Intranet, with the potential to house future services. Students can manage their projects and online profile, while teachers can collect their students' project documentation.

Interaction Standards

Header

The header is a full width orange-red bar. It is slightly overlapped by the hero or sidekick image. On the left end, contained within the grid, is the school's logo. Flush to the right edge is the top-level navigation.

Heroes and Sidekicks

Below the logo and navigation is a large panoramic image, the taller of which are heroes and the shorter of which are sidekicks.

Heroes are visible only on the homepage and the top-level Exhibition page, and are for critically important announcements (such as Confluence and the Lecture Series) as well as cultural highlight stories.

Sidekicks are present on all other pages, and should contain imagery representative of the page's content.

Features Bar

This design also incorporates a features bar on the homepage for high quality content from the community. These can include original videos, community profiles, or important articles.

Featured content are meant to provide lasting value, and therefore do not flow temporally with the news. More general news, like what is frequently on the site as it exists now, is placed in a column below this bar, alongside events and student projects.

Side Navigation with Breadcrumbs

Primarily a content page convention, side navigation appears to help the user navigate into deeper levels of information. There are states for both second-level and third-level links in this element, and the site architecture must not dive deeper than three levels of hierarchy on any content branch.

In most cases, the side navigation appears with a breadcrumb trail to help the user navigate back up the content branch. In the Exhibition, breadcrumb trails occur independently of side navigation.

Bottom Bar

Studies have shown that when a user reaches the end of a page, if there isn't compelling enough information, they'll leave the site entirely. The bottom bar is an element whose primary purpose is to increase user engagement by weaving a consistent story throughout branches of content.

This element shows a variety of navigation options, for instance, a link to the previous and next news stories. In content pages, it continues to the next page in that content branch. In this way, this element weaves smaller parts of the entire website together and helps encourage the user to learn the entire story of our school.

Related Content

On every page, except for the homepage and Exhibition, this bar provides 2–4 pieces of related content. For example, on a page about applying to the school, it can house quick links for downloading relevant PDF forms.

When the related content box ends, allow it some vertical clearance before "breaking the grid" into it's vertical column, to help the site read clearly.
