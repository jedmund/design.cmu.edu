Overview

This page is documentation for the interaction prototype for a new School of Design website and intranet.

The intent of this page is to highlight insights that we learned over the course of a semester researching the school and its constituencies, as well as document the various interaction and visual standards that we established and our thought processes behind them.

At the bottom of the page is a diagram of which pages are presently functional in this prototype. Thusly, that diagram may, and probably will, change over time.

Takeaways

  • Importance of Information

    The website is the main source of information about the school and should be treated as a priority.

    Information should always manifest in the format that it wants to be in. Each piece of information should be treated as its own designed deliverable.

    The web is capable of good typography and hierarchy and should reflect the aesthetic values of the school.

  • Process Culture

    The importance of process and documentation should be indoctrinated in the students. They present narratives of thought, not necessarily final products.

    We aren't creating a gallery because we present stories, not slideshows.

    The new gallery structure isn't creating more work, it's a natural step forward from physical process books. It's more environmental and accessible for everyone.

  • Involving the Community

    All parties we've spoken to agree that there is not enough transparency and overlap between different groups within the school.

    There's also not enough transparency with alumni interested in our progress. Everyone should be a part of one conversation.

    Given an outlet, students will create high-quality content that drives internal, and consequently external, culture.

Interaction Standards

Header

The header is a full width orange-red bar. It is slightly overlapped by the hero or sidekick image, by 30px to be exact.

On the left end, contained within the grid, is the school's logo. Flush to the right edge is the top-level navigation.

Heroes and Sidekicks

The website features a full-width header with horizontal navigation. Below the logo and navigation is a large panoramic image, the taller of which are heroes and the shorter of which are sidekicks.

Heroes are visible only on the homepage and the top-level Exhibition page, and are reserved for very time-intensive events or cultural stories. Sidekicks are present on all other pages, and should contain imagery representative of the page's content.

The hero image is 355px tall, and a sidekick is about half that size at 175px tall. They are both 934px wide.

Features Bar

This design also incorporates a features bar on the homepage for other culturally-significant articles. These can include personal stories, original videos, and exceptional projects. Content that falls into this category should sufficiently answer the question, "What is design?"

Featured content are meant to provide lasting value, and therefore do not flow temporally with the news. More general news, like what is frequently on the site as it exists now, is placed in a column below this bar, alongside events and more generally selected, if not random, projects.

Images in this bar are 300px by 180px.

Side Navigation with Breadcrumbs

Primarily a content page convention, side navigation appears to help the user navigate into deeper levels of information. There are states for both second-level and third-level links in this element, and the site architecture must not dive deeper than three levels of hierarchy on any content branch.

In most, if not all cases, the side navigation appears with a breadcrumb trail to help the user navigate back up the content branch. In the Exhibition, breadcrumb trails occur independently of side navigation.

Bottom Bar

Studies have shown that when a user reaches the end of a page, if there isn't compelling enough information, they'll leave the site entirely. The bottom bar is an element whose primary purpose is to increase user engagement by weaving a consistent story throughout branches of content.

This element shows a variety of navigation options, for instance, a link to the previous and next news stories. In content pages, it continues to the next page in that content branch. In this way, this element weaves smaller parts of the entire website together and helps encourage the user to learn the entire story of our school.

Related Content

We've transformed the random news selector from the current website into a recurring element to house related content. On every page, except for the homepage and exhibition pages, this bar appears to aid the user in finding related content. For example, on a page about applying to the school, it can house quick links for downloading relevant PDF forms.

It should contain minimum two items, and at absolute maximum, four. If there is an image inside of an item, its dimensions should be 190px wide by 90px tall.
